Welcome to Jonathan Zavialov's Virtual Museum. This exhibit focuses on the earthquake in Sparta in 464 BCE. You will learn about its immediate effects, as well as its lasting influence on Ancient Greek history, including its context in the Peloponnesian War.

The Setting

Within Ancient Greece, tensions were very high during the 460s BCE. Some of the most powerful cities were Athens and Sparta. Only 20 years earlier, they had fought together in the Persian War. Notably, in the battle at Thermopylae and Salamis, Spartan troops bravely delayed the Persians, giving Athens enough time to evacuate its cities to safety. (Zhou 107)

However, over the next two decades, the two cities grew increasingly opposed to each other. The two cities had many differences, which may have contributed. Athens was a democracy, while Sparta was an oligarchy. Athens was a maritime power, while Sparta was a land power. Athens was a city-state, while Sparta was a military state. Athens was a city of culture, while Sparta was a city of war. Athens was a city of the people, while Sparta was a city of the elite.

In these 20 years, the Delian League was created. Many cities were part of it, but notably, not Sparta. With the progression of time, Athens became increasingly more powerful. As some cities began to leave, Athens became the superpower within the league, taking over control of much of Greece in the process. Tensions became even higher when Sparta invaded Attica, the region that Athens was located in.

The Earthquake

The earthquake occurred in 464 BCE. It was very strong, having a magnitude of 7.2. To put this into perspective, the earthquake that happened in Haiti in 2010, killing over 200,000, had a magnitude of 7. According to ancient historian Thucydides, over 20,000 people died in the Spartan earthquake. However, modern historians say this might have been an exaggeration.

The Helots were the working class of Sparta, often working in the farms within the surrounding area. They were often treated poorly, only being allowed to live in the city if they were deemed fit enough to work. The earthquake left the city in chaos, with the Helots taking advantage of the situation. They revolted, taking control of Sparta.

Because of the revolt, Sparta requested Athens's help to regain control of the city. In response, Athens sent 4,000 soldiers to help. However, on their way to Sparta, something happened which led to Sparta refusing them. Historians are unsure of exactly what happened, but regardless of what it was, it was enough to cause Sparta to refuse Athens' help (Zhou 108). Sparta's refusal was seen as an act of hostility towards Athens, which was one of the key factors in causing the First Peloponnesian war.
